The Seymour Second Neighborhood Conjecture (SSNC) claims that every oriented graph has a node whose second out neighborhood is at least as large as its first.

Most proof attempts at this conjecture have been probabilistic or worked on specific classes of graphs.

To truly tackle this problem, we needed something that could operate on every oriented graph. The perfect tool for the job was the Breadth First Search (BFS) algorithm. Combining this algorithm with lexicographic ordering gives a coordinate system, or a well-ordering on the nodes of a graph.

With this, we can use a Minimum Counterexample Environment (MCE) to prove the SSNC. The necessity in this environment is that every node is non-Seymour (i.e. has a first neighborhood larger than its second). To ensure this requires that children form cycles within the neighborhoods.

These are the well studied transitive triangles that have received a lot of attention on this problem. A parent u connects to two children v and w> and v connects to w. By these children forming a cycle in the parent's first neighborhood, they are able to have one less second neighbor. This reduces the parent's second neighborhood, allowing that parent to be non-Seymour.

Further, if a child has a higher out-degree than required, this does not help in preventing the parent become a non-Seymour vertex. Thus thse extra-interior arcs are unnexessary in an MCE environment.

Similarly, back arcs only visit previously optimized neighborhoods which are not helpful in an MCE environment because these back arcs will instantly create a Seymour vertex.

What we are left with is

  • We have a strict \delta-regular graph in this MCE environment.
  • The neighborhood R_k at distance k must be k-regular.
  • By the definition of $k$-regularity implies Eulerian graphs. This says we have edge disjoint cycles inside these neighbodhoods.
  • These non-Seymour nodes show that the neighborhoods are decreasing in size (R_k = \delta - k + 1)
  • The binomial theorem gives a bound on how many of these neighborhoods can occur (bounded by \delta / 3).
